Abstract

Hydrostatic pressure monitoring is critical for maritime applications including environmental inspections, early detection of underwater earthquakes and tsunamis, and offshore energy systems. This research proposes an FBG-based hydrostatic pressure sensor constructed using a PVC pressure chamber with the FBG affixed to the underside of a thin 304 stainless steel diaphragm (60 mm diameter, 2 mm thick) clamped at the edge. Pressure was applied from 1–5 kg/cm² corresponding to ~48.7 m depth in the epipelagic zone. Results demonstrated first-order sensor behaviour with sensitivity of 12.09 pm/(kg/cm²) and R² = 0.9679 during loading, and 12.66 pm/(kg/cm²) with R² = 0.9571 during unloading. Full-range testing reached R² = 0.9945 at 12.86 pm/(kg/cm²).

Background

Why Monitor Hydrostatic Pressure in Indonesia?

Indonesia — the world's largest archipelagic state — has a coastline exceeding 108,000 km and lies at the convergence of three major tectonic plates. It records over 320 earthquakes of magnitude ≥ 5.0 annually, most at shallow depths below 70 km, making its coastal regions highly vulnerable to tsunamis.

The Ina-CBT (Indonesia Cable-Based Tsunameter), deployed in Labuan Bajo since 2022 by BRIN, uses quartz-based MEMS pressure recorders. While sensitive, MEMS sensors are susceptible to electromagnetic interference (EMI) and are complex for multi-point deployment. FBG-based sensors address these limitations directly — immune to EMI, corrosion-resistant, and capable of multiplexed sensing across a single fiber.

Research context: The Java Sea averages 40–46 m depth, with over 65% of the region shallower than 50 m — placing it squarely in the epipelagic zone (0–200 m). This sensor targets exactly this operational range.

Theory

Fiber Bragg Grating — Working Principle

A Fiber Bragg Grating (FBG) is formed by inducing periodic refractive index variations along the core of an optical fiber. This structure selectively reflects a narrow wavelength band — the Bragg wavelength — while transmitting all others. When the fiber is subjected to strain or temperature change, the grating period shifts, causing a measurable change in the reflected wavelength.

This research uses a Uniform FBG (UFBG) inscribed on SMF-28e single-mode fiber — the most common type, producing a sharp, symmetric reflection peak ideal for pressure sensing. The sensor was bonded to the center of a stainless steel diaphragm; pressure-induced diaphragm deflection creates axial strain on the FBG, shifting λB.

Key Equations

The equations below are rendered as text for accessibility. For exact typeset form, refer to the published paper (Springer LNEE, ICNERE 2026).

Eq. 1 — Pressure Definition

Fluid Pressure

P = F / A

Pressure as the ratio of normal force to area. SI unit: Pascal (Pa).

Eq. 2 — Hydrostatic Pressure

Pressure at Depth h

P = P₀ + ρgh

P₀ = atmospheric pressure, ρ = fluid density, g = gravitational acceleration [16].

Eq. 3 — Bragg Condition

Bragg Wavelength

λ_B = 2 · n_eff · Λ

λ_B = Bragg wavelength, n_eff = effective refractive index, Λ = grating period [22–30].

Eq. 4 — Wavelength Shift

Full Thermo-Mechanical Shift

Δλ_B / λ_B = (1 - p_e)·Δε + (α + ζ)·ΔT

p_e = photo-elastic coefficient, Δε = axial strain, α = thermal expansion, ζ = thermo-optic coefficient [31–35].

Eq. 5 — Isothermal Shift

Strain-Only (ΔT = 0)

Δλ_B = λ_B · (1 - p_e) · Δε

Simplified when temperature is constant — applies directly to this experiment at stable 25 °C lab conditions.

Eq. 13 — Flexural Rigidity

Diaphragm Stiffness

D = E·h³ / [12(1 - ν²)]

E = Young's modulus, h = thickness, ν = Poisson's ratio. Governs resistance to bending [36].

Eq. 14 — Max Deflection

Clamped Circular Diaphragm

w_max = q·a⁴ / (64·D)

q = uniform pressure, a = diaphragm radius. Gives centre deflection for clamped-edge condition [36,37].

Eq. 15 — Centre Strain

Radial Strain at r = 0

ε_r(0) = -3(1-ν)·q·a² / (4·E·h²)

Strain at the diaphragm centre where the FBG is bonded. Directly drives wavelength shift.

Eq. 16 — Pressure → Wavelength

FBG Bragg Shift from Pressure

Δλ_B = λ_B·(1-p_e)·[-3(1-ν)·q·a²/(4Eh²)]

Key design equation — links applied pressure q to measurable wavelength shift Δλ_B.

Eq. 17 — Inverse: Pressure

Recover Pressure from Δλ_B

q = Δλ_B · 4Eh² / [λ_B·(1-p_e)·3(1-ν)·a²]

Allows the sensor to directly report pressure from a measured wavelength shift. Core of the transduction chain.

Methodology

Experimental Setup & Procedure

All experiments were conducted at the Electronics and Instrumentation Laboratory, Research Centre for Industrial Process and Manufacturing Technology, BRIN, Serpong, Banten (August 2024 – June 2025).

1
Design & Simulation Diaphragm deflection simulated using ANSYS Structural. Analytical validation via Kirchhoff-Love plate theory (Eq. 14–15). Material: 304 stainless steel (E = 193 GPa, ν = 0.29, yield strength 215 MPa).
2
Sensor Fabrication Bare uniform FBG (λ_B = 1550.056 nm, BW = 0.18 nm, reflectance 90.55%) spliced with FC/APC pigtail via fusion splicer. Bonded to diaphragm centre using structural adhesive.
3
Chamber Assembly 2-inch AW PVC pipe chamber sealed with Loctite 577. 304 SS diaphragm (60 mm dia., 2 mm thick) clamped at flange. Optical attenuator (0–12 dB) installed to prevent saturation.
4
Pressure Testing Chamber filled with water. Pressure applied via manual hydraulic pump in increments from 1–5 kg/cm² (then extended to 10 kg/cm²). Both loading and unloading phases recorded.
5
Data Acquisition & Analysis Bragg wavelength acquired via I-MON 512 USB interrogator (range 1510–1595 nm, resolution <0.5 pm) at 0.5-second intervals. Weighted Least Squares (WLS) regression applied to address heteroscedasticity.

Results

Experimental Findings

The FBG sensor exhibited consistent first-order dynamic behaviour across all nine steady-state tests. The Bragg wavelength stabilised within approximately 80–120 seconds after each pressure step, consistent with a damped first-order instrument response. The time derivative of the wavelength shift followed an exponential decay, confirming no overshoot and smooth convergence to equilibrium.

The loading regression yielded: λ_B = 0.01209·P + 1550.056 (nm), where P is in kg/cm². The unloading regression: λ_B = 0.01266·P + 1550.042. The intercept offset between loading and unloading indicates hysteresis, attributed to residual strain relaxation in the diaphragm after bolted clamping during assembly.

Efficiency finding: Full-range testing revealed only 28.25% transduction efficiency compared to theoretical sensitivity. The discrepancy is attributed to bonding compliance (adhesive layer between FBG and diaphragm), splice loss in the optical path, and bending-induced attenuation. This highlights the importance of rigid bonding techniques and optimised splice quality in FBG pressure sensors.

Conclusion

Summary & Contribution

This research demonstrated that an FBG bonded to a 304 stainless steel circular diaphragm provides a reliable, repeatable hydrostatic pressure measurement suitable for shallow marine environments. The sensor exhibited linear first-order behaviour across all tested pressure ranges, with high linearity (R² up to 0.9945).

Key contributions: (1) complete analytical model linking hydrostatic pressure to Bragg wavelength shift via plate theory (Eq. 16–17), (2) ANSYS-validated diaphragm design, (3) experimental characterisation of sensitivity, hysteresis, and transduction efficiency in a controlled laboratory setting, and (4) demonstration of sensor applicability for Indonesia's epipelagic coastal monitoring context.

This work was published as a Springer Lecture Notes in Electrical Engineering (LNEE) paper at the 6th International Conference on Nano Electronics Research and Education (ICNERE 2026), presented in Hamamatsu, Shizuoka, Japan.
